\begin{abstract}
Production deployments of large language models increasingly route queries
across a cascade of cheap, mid, and frontier engines to balance cost against
quality. Recent work (Dekoninck, Baader, and Vechev, 2025) derives the
\emph{per-query optimal} routing-and-cascading rule \emph{given} a trustworthy
quality estimator, and identifies estimator quality as the decisive factor.
We address the orthogonal problem their analysis assumes away: when the
per-engine quality and reliability signal is \emph{unknown a priori} and
\emph{non-stationary}---drifting with load, time-of-day, and model
deprecation---how fast can a router \emph{learn} to match the dynamic
optimum? We give Contextual Discounted Thompson Sampling (CD-TS), an online
algorithm with a non-stationary pseudo-regret bound of
$\widetilde{O}\!\big((MK\log T)^{1/3}\, V_T^{1/3}\, T^{2/3}\big)$ against the
dynamic optimum, matching the Besbes--Gur--Zeevi lower bound up to log
factors. The two results compose: their rule is the per-step oracle our
regret is measured against. Empirically, in a controlled-reward
non-stationary bandit with three drift regimes
(one-shot collapse, monotone drift, periodic) the measured regret-vs-horizon
exponent of CD-TS is $0.20$--$0.55$ across regimes---comfortably within the
$2/3$ upper bound---versus a frozen offline-optimal comparator that scales
linearly, a $25$--$33{\times}$ degradation under abrupt and drift regimes.
Because a known variation budget is unrealistic in production, we further give
a $V_T$-free \emph{detect-and-restart} variant (Adaptive CD-TS) that pairs a
mild discount with a per-arm change detector; with no access to $V_T$ it
matches or beats the oracle-tuned discount on final dynamic regret in all three
regimes. We release Cascade Engine, a reference implementation with reproducible
manifests pinning code, data, and toolchain versions, alongside the formal
benchmarking protocol (real baseline checkpoints, reward-model quality axis,
Pareto-frontier scoring, $t$-distribution confidence intervals at the actual
seed count).
\end{abstract}

\section{Introduction}
\label{sec:intro}

Multi-tier LLM serving is now standard practice. A request enters a
\emph{router} that selects, or escalates through, a sequence of progressively
more capable (and more expensive) engines---typically a local model, a mid
cloud model, and a frontier model. The literature now offers two well
understood static treatments: \emph{routing} (pick one model per query,
e.g.\ RouteLLM~\cite{ong2024routellm}) and \emph{cascading} (sequentially
escalate, e.g.\ FrugalGPT~\cite{chen2023frugalgpt}). A 2025 result by
Dekoninck, Baader, and Vechev~\cite{dekoninck2025unified}, hereafter
\textbf{DBV-25}, unifies the two: given a quality estimator
$\widehat{Q}_{x,a}$ with a known uncertainty covariance and a cost estimator
$\widehat{c}_a$, they characterize the per-query optimal cascade-routing
rule and show that estimator quality is the decisive factor governing
realized cost--quality.

DBV-25 effectively closes the \emph{decision-theoretic} half of the problem
under a stationary world with a trustworthy estimator. The open half---and
the regime that dominates production deployment---is the opposite: the
quality/reliability signal of cloud engines \emph{drifts}. Tail latencies
shift hour-by-hour with shared infrastructure load. Hosted models are
deprecated or silently re-snapshotted. New context-length or safety filters
are deployed mid-week. A frozen estimator calibrated yesterday is no longer
trustworthy today.

Concretely, DBV-25 names good quality estimators as the critical input but
takes them as given; our concern is precisely how a router should behave
when that input must be \emph{learned online while it drifts}.

\paragraph{Contribution.}
We pose multi-tier LLM routing as a non-stationary contextual stochastic
bandit and give a sharp characterization of the achievable rate:

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*, itemsep=2pt, topsep=2pt]
\item \textbf{Algorithm.} Contextual Discounted Thompson Sampling
(\textsc{CD-TS}, \S\ref{sec:algorithm})---a per-context discounted
Beta-Bernoulli posterior with the Agrawal--Goyal Bernoulli trick. The
trick is essential: a heuristic update $\alpha \mathrel{+}= r\, \one{r>1/2}$
is biased and breaks Beta--Bernoulli conjugacy; we document the failure
mode in \S\ref{sec:impl-note} because the previous repository
implementation made exactly this error.

\item \textbf{Regret bound (Theorem~\ref{thm:main}).} With the discount
$1-\gamma = (MK\log T)^{1/3}(V_T/T)^{2/3}$, CD-TS attains expected dynamic
regret
$\E[R_T] \leq C(MK\log T)^{1/3} V_T^{1/3} T^{2/3}$, where $M$ is the
number of complexity contexts, $K$ the number of engines, $V_T$ the
total-variation budget on the reward means. This matches the
Besbes--Gur--Zeevi~\cite{besbes2014stochastic} lower bound
$\Omega(K^{1/3}V_T^{1/3}T^{2/3})$ up to a $\log^{1/3} T$ factor.

\item \textbf{Empirical validation of the rate
(\S\ref{sec:experiments}).} Across three controlled drift regimes
(abrupt, monotone drift, periodic) and five seeds, with $V_T$ pinned to
within $0.1\%$ over horizons $T \in \{2,4,8,16\}\!\times\!10^3$, the
measured \emph{cross-horizon} regret exponent is $0.35/0.20/0.55$ for
CD-TS versus $1.00/1.00/0.75$ for the frozen offline-optimal comparator
that instantiates DBV-25's calibrate-once-and-commit paradigm. CD-TS is
sublinear in every regime; the frozen oracle is essentially linear under
abrupt or monotone drift.

\item \textbf{$V_T$-free adaptive variant (\S\ref{sec:adaptive}).} Since
Theorem~\ref{thm:main} tunes $\gamma$ from a variation budget $V_T$ that a
production router does not know in advance, we give Adaptive CD-TS
(Algorithm~\ref{alg:adaptive}): a mild fixed discount for gradual drift plus a
per-arm two-sided CUSUM change detector~\cite{liu2018change,cao2019nearly}
that restarts an arm's posterior on an abrupt break. With \emph{no} access to
$V_T$ it matches or beats the oracle-tuned CD-TS on final dynamic regret in all
three regimes ($46$ vs $104$ on abrupt, $83$ vs $84$ on drift, $94$ vs $163$
on periodic at $T{=}16\mathrm{k}$), a deployable alternative to the
bandit-over-bandit meta-learner of~\cite{cheung2019learning}.

\item \textbf{Honest MDP-router demarcation (\S\ref{sec:mdp}).} We also
state---and bound---an MDP variant with a tabular Q-learning policy.
Instantiating the Azar--Osband--Munos and Jin et al.\ bounds at our exact
state geometry ($|\mathcal{S}|=1000$, $|\mathcal{A}|=4$) shows that even
the \emph{best possible} model-free episodic-RL algorithm needs
${\geq}10^7$ episodes for an $\varepsilon{=}0.1$ policy, far more than any
production router will ever see. We therefore demarcate the MDP variant as
an empirically-motivated heuristic with no competitive finite-sample
guarantee at realistic horizons.

\item \textbf{Reproducibility and benchmarking protocol
(\S\ref{sec:protocol}).} We use released baseline checkpoints
(RouteLLM's \texttt{bert\_gpt4\_1106\_augmented} and a public reward model
as the FrugalGPT scorer), a reward-model quality axis independent of the
router's self-report, true Pareto-frontier scoring, $t$-distribution CIs
at the actual seed count, and a uniformly-applied orchestration wrapper
so caching/PII layers cannot leak into one method's pipeline alone. Every
run emits a manifest pinning git SHA, dataset SHA-256, package versions,
and seed list.
\end{enumerate}

\paragraph{What this paper is not.}
We do not contest the static optimality of DBV-25. Their per-query rule is
the right target when a trustworthy estimator is available; it is the
oracle our regret is measured against. We do not claim that CD-TS or its
MDP cousin is the right algorithm for every deployment---small-scale
applications without drift should use DBV-25 directly. We make the narrower
claim that under drift, no-regret learning is provably necessary and
achievable.

\section{Related Work}
\label{sec:related}

\paragraph{Routing: one model per query.}
Routing methods train a predictor that maps a query to a single model.
RouteLLM~\cite{ong2024routellm} learns a binary strong/weak router from
Chatbot Arena preference data; we reproduce its released BERT checkpoint as
a baseline rather than a proxy. Hybrid LLM~\cite{ding2024hybrid} trains a
router on a quality gap signal and exposes a cost knob; ZOOTER~\cite{lu2023zooter}
distills reward-model supervision into a lightweight router;
RouterBench~\cite{hu2024routerbench} standardizes evaluation across
routers; FORC~\cite{sakota2024forc} frames routing as meta-model
assignment. All of these are \emph{static}: the predictor is trained
offline and frozen.

\paragraph{Cascading: sequential escalation.}
FrugalGPT~\cite{chen2023frugalgpt} introduced the LLM-cascade with a
learned scorer triggering escalation; we reproduce this with a public
reward model rather than a hand-tuned heuristic.
AutoMix~\cite{madaan2023automix} uses self-verification plus a meta-verifier
to decide escalation. Prior to 2025, cascading lacked optimality proofs and
could not be combined with routing in a principled way.

\paragraph{Unified routing and cascading.}
The closest work to ours is DBV-25~\cite{dekoninck2025unified}. They
derive a per-query optimal cascading strategy, prove optimality of an
existing routing strategy, and unify both into \emph{cascade routing},
identifying quality-estimator quality as the decisive factor.
Their result is decision-theoretic and \emph{static}: given a quality
estimator with a known uncertainty covariance and a cost estimator, they
characterize the optimal one-shot model-selection rule, with
hyperparameters fit offline by Bayesian search over pre-computed responses.

\paragraph{Our delta.}
We adopt as a baseline the static optimality of DBV-25: their per-query
rule is the right target when a trustworthy quality estimator is available
and the environment is stationary. Our contribution is orthogonal and
addresses precisely the question their framework takes as given---good
estimators. We study the regime in which the per-engine
quality/reliability signal is unknown a priori and non-stationary. CD-TS
gives a non-stationary pseudo-regret bound against the \emph{dynamic}
optimum with a matching lower bound. Informally, DBV-25 characterize
\emph{what} the optimal decision is given an estimator; we characterize
\emph{how fast} an agent provably converges to good decisions while
\emph{learning} that estimator online under drift. The two results
compose---their rule is the per-step oracle our regret is measured
against---and neither subsumes the other.

\paragraph{Non-stationary bandits and RL.}
Our analysis instantiates the discounted/sliding-window non-stationary
bandit program. Garivier and Moulines~\cite{garivier2008upper} established
the $T^{2/3}$ regime for switching bandits via discounted/windowed UCB.
Besbes, Gur, and Zeevi~\cite{besbes2014stochastic} gave the
variation-budget formulation and the matching
$\Omega(V_T^{1/3}T^{2/3})$ lower bound. Russac, Vernade, and
Capp{\'e}~\cite{russac2019weighted} and Trov{\`o}, Restelli, and
Gatti~\cite{trovo2020sliding} extended weighted and sliding-window
estimators to linear and Thompson settings. Cheung, Simchi-Levi, and
Zhu~\cite{cheung2019learning} removed the need to know $V_T$ via a
bandit-over-bandit meta-algorithm. For the MDP variant we reference
\cite{azar2017minimax,jin2018qlearning,domingues2021episodic}. Our
technical novelty is the \emph{instantiation} of this machinery for LLM
cascade routing: the contextual structure is the prompt-complexity
binning, the arms are engine tiers, the reward is the cost--quality
composite scored by an external reward model, and the non-stationarity is
the empirically-observed drift in cloud-LLM reliability.

\section{Problem Setup}
\label{sec:setup}

We model multi-tier LLM routing as a non-stationary, contextual stochastic
bandit. At each round $t \in \{1,\dots,T\}$:
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*, itemsep=1pt, topsep=2pt]
\item Nature reveals a context $x_t \in \mathcal{X}$ (a discrete
\emph{complexity bin} of the incoming prompt; $|\mathcal{X}| = M < \infty$).
\item The router selects an arm $a_t \in \mathcal{A} = \{1,\dots,K\}$ (one
of $K$ engine tiers).
\item Nature draws a reward $r_t \sim \nu_{x_t, a_t, t}$ supported on
$[0,1]$ with mean $\mu_{x,a,t}$.
\end{enumerate}

The reward is the cost--quality composite used in the Cascade Engine
implementation:
$r_t = \big(q_t - \lambda c_t - \mu_l \ell_t\big)_{[0,1]}$, where
$q_t \in [0,1]$ is the reward-model quality score on the engine's response,
$c_t$ is monetary cost, $\ell_t$ is latency, and $\lambda, \mu_l > 0$ are
weights. Since $c_t$ is deterministic given $a_t$ (engines have fixed
per-token prices) and $q_t, \ell_t$ are bounded, $r_t \in [0,1]$
deterministically.

\paragraph{Non-stationarity.}
Engine reliability and quality drift over time. We allow arbitrary changes
to $\mu_{x,a,t}$ subject to a total-variation budget:
\begin{equation}
V_T \;:=\; \sum_{t=2}^{T} \sum_{x \in \mathcal{X}} \sum_{a \in \mathcal{A}} \big|\mu_{x,a,t} - \mu_{x,a,t-1}\big|.
\label{eq:variation-budget}
\end{equation}

\paragraph{Performance metric.}
Define the dynamic optimal action
$a^\star_{x,t} := \argmax_{a \in \mathcal{A}} \mu_{x,a,t}$. The pseudo-regret
against the \emph{dynamic} oracle is
\begin{equation}
R_T \;:=\; \sum_{t=1}^{T} \big(\mu_{x_t, a^\star_{x_t, t}, t} - \mu_{x_t, a_t, t}\big).
\label{eq:regret-def}
\end{equation}
We measure against the dynamic oracle, not a single best-in-hindsight arm:
in the LLM-routing setting the same engine that was best at noon may be
worst at midnight, and a fixed comparator would mask the cost of failing to
react.

\section{Algorithm: Contextual Discounted Thompson Sampling}
\label{sec:algorithm}

We analyze the Bernoulli-trick variant of Thompson sampling with geometric
discounting of past observations on the \emph{played arm only}. The
Bernoulli trick (\cite{agrawal2012analysis}, \S2) preserves the conjugacy
of Beta posteriors under continuous bounded rewards by randomly rounding
each $r_t$ to $\{0,1\}$ with success probability $r_t$.

\begin{algorithm}[h]
\caption{Contextual Discounted Thompson Sampling (CD-TS)}
\label{alg:cd-ts}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\Require Discount factor $\gamma \in (0,1)$; floor parameter $\eta \geq 1$.
\State Initialize $\alpha_{x,a,0} \gets 1$, $\beta_{x,a,0} \gets 1$ for all $(x,a)$.
\For{$t = 1, 2, \dots, T$}
    \State Observe context $x_t$.
    \For{$a \in \mathcal{A}$}
        \State Sample $\theta_{x_t,a,t} \sim \Beta(\alpha_{x_t,a,t-1}, \beta_{x_t,a,t-1})$.
    \EndFor
    \State Play $a_t \gets \argmax_a \theta_{x_t,a,t}$ and observe reward $r_t \in [0,1]$.
    \State Bernoulli trick: draw $y_t \sim \Bern(r_t)$.
    \State $\alpha_{x_t,a_t,t} \gets \max\{\eta, \gamma \cdot \alpha_{x_t,a_t,t-1} + y_t\}$
    \State $\beta_{x_t,a_t,t} \gets \max\{\eta, \gamma \cdot \beta_{x_t,a_t,t-1} + (1-y_t)\}$
    \For{every other $(x',a') \neq (x_t,a_t)$}
        \State $\alpha_{x',a',t} \gets \alpha_{x',a',t-1}$, $\beta_{x',a',t} \gets \beta_{x',a',t-1}$
    \EndFor
\EndFor
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\begin{remark}[Why discount only the played arm]
\label{rem:per-arm-decay}
A prior version of this code decayed every arm at every step
(``global'' decay). This is appealing but theoretically wrong: it
accelerates information loss on arms that are not being played, with no
corresponding regret benefit, and breaks the per-arm martingale used in
the proof. Per-played-arm decay is standard
\cite{russac2019weighted,trovo2020sliding} and is what
Theorem~\ref{thm:main} proves a bound for.
\end{remark}

\section{Regret Analysis}
\label{sec:theory}

\begin{assumption}[Bounded variation budget]
\label{ass:budget}
The variation $V_T$ in \eqref{eq:variation-budget} is finite and known to
the algorithm at training time.
\end{assumption}

When $V_T$ is not known, the bandit-over-bandit construction of Cheung,
Simchi-Levi, and Zhu~\cite{cheung2019learning} recovers the same rate
adaptively up to logarithmic factors.

\begin{theorem}[Regret of CD-TS in the non-stationary contextual setting]
\label{thm:main}
Under Assumption~\ref{ass:budget}, for any horizon $T \geq 2$, if
Algorithm~\ref{alg:cd-ts} is run with discount factor
\[
1 - \gamma \;=\; \min\!\Big\{ 1,\; (M K \log T )^{1/3}(V_T / T)^{2/3}\Big\}
\]
then the expected pseudo-regret satisfies
\begin{equation}
\E[R_T] \;\leq\; C \cdot (M K \log T)^{1/3} \cdot V_T^{1/3} \cdot T^{2/3}
\label{eq:main-bound}
\end{equation}
for a universal constant $C > 0$.
\end{theorem}

\paragraph{Interpretation.}
For the Cascade Engine deployment $M = 5$ complexity bins and
$K \in \{2,3\}$ engines, so $MK \leq 15$ is a small constant. The
asymptotic rate $\widetilde{O}(V_T^{1/3}T^{2/3})$ matches the minimax lower
bound for non-stationary multi-armed bandits up to logarithmic
factors~\cite{garivier2008upper,besbes2014stochastic}.

\paragraph{Stationary corollary.}
If the environment is stationary ($V_T = 0$), Algorithm~\ref{alg:cd-ts}
with $\gamma = 1$ reduces to standard contextual Thompson sampling, and
\cite{agrawal2013further} gives the sharper problem-independent bound
$\E[R_T] = O(\sqrt{MKT \log T})$, with a problem-dependent bound
$\E[R_T] = O\!\big(\sum_{x,a} \log T / \Delta_{x,a}\big)$
(sum over $(x,a)$ with $\Delta_{x,a} > 0$), where $\Delta_{x,a}$ is the
gap to the best arm in context $x$.

\subsection{Proof}

The proof partitions $[T]$ into intervals of length
$L := \lceil 1/(1-\gamma) \rceil$ (the effective memory window of the
discount), bounds the regret on each interval against a fixed
(per-interval) reward distribution, and accounts for the drift bias
introduced by treating the interval as stationary.

Fix an interval $\mathcal{I} = [s, s+L) \subseteq [T]$. Define the
windowed mean for context $x$ and arm $a$:
$\bar{\mu}_{x,a}^{\mathcal{I}} := \frac{1}{L} \sum_{t \in \mathcal{I}} \mu_{x,a,t}$.
By the triangle inequality and \eqref{eq:variation-budget}, for any
$t, t' \in \mathcal{I}$:
\[
\big|\mu_{x,a,t} - \mu_{x,a,t'}\big| \;\leq\; V^{\mathcal{I}}_{x,a},
\quad
V^{\mathcal{I}}_{x,a} := \sum_{\tau \in \mathcal{I}}\big|\mu_{x,a,\tau} - \mu_{x,a,\tau-1}\big|.
\]

\begin{lemma}[Drift regret on one interval]
\label{lem:drift}
Let $\widetilde{R}_{\mathcal{I}}$ denote the regret on $\mathcal{I}$
against the \emph{best fixed-in-interval} arm,
$\widetilde{R}_{\mathcal{I}} := \sum_{t \in \mathcal{I}} \big(\bar{\mu}^{\mathcal{I}}_{x_t, a^\dagger_{x_t}} - \mu_{x_t, a_t, t}\big)$
with $a^\dagger_x := \argmax_a \bar{\mu}^{\mathcal{I}}_{x,a}$. Define
$V^{\mathcal{I}} := \max_{x,a} V^{\mathcal{I}}_{x,a}$. Then the dynamic regret on $\mathcal{I}$ satisfies
\begin{equation}
\sum_{t \in \mathcal{I}}\big(\mu_{x_t, a^\star_{x_t, t}, t} - \mu_{x_t, a_t, t}\big) \;\leq\; \widetilde{R}_{\mathcal{I}} + 2 L \cdot V^{\mathcal{I}}.
\label{eq:lem-drift}
\end{equation}
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}
Decompose the instantaneous dynamic regret at $t \in \mathcal{I}$ as
\begin{align*}
\mu_{x_t, a^\star_{x_t,t}, t} - \mu_{x_t, a_t, t}
&= \underbrace{\big[\mu_{x_t, a^\star_{x_t,t}, t} - \bar{\mu}^{\mathcal{I}}_{x_t, a^\star_{x_t,t}}\big]}_{\text{(i): single-arm drift}}
+ \underbrace{\big[\bar{\mu}^{\mathcal{I}}_{x_t, a^\star_{x_t,t}} - \bar{\mu}^{\mathcal{I}}_{x_t, a^\dagger_{x_t}}\big]}_{\text{(ii) $\leq 0$}}\\
&\quad + \underbrace{\big[\bar{\mu}^{\mathcal{I}}_{x_t, a^\dagger_{x_t}} - \mu_{x_t, a_t, t}\big]}_{\text{(iii): stationary-regret summand}}.
\end{align*}
Term (ii) is non-positive by definition of $a^\dagger$. Term (iii) sums
to $\widetilde{R}_{\mathcal{I}}$. For term (i), the within-interval drift
bound gives $|\mu_{x,a,t} - \bar{\mu}^{\mathcal{I}}_{x,a}| \leq V^{\mathcal{I}}$;
summing over the $L$ rounds gives at most $L \cdot V^{\mathcal{I}}$. A symmetric
argument applies to the played-arm summand, contributing another
$L\cdot V^{\mathcal{I}}$.
\end{proof}

\begin{lemma}[Stationary regret on one interval]
\label{lem:stationary-interval}
For Algorithm~\ref{alg:cd-ts} run with $\gamma$ satisfying
$L = \lceil 1/(1-\gamma) \rceil$, the expected regret against the best
fixed-in-interval arm on $\mathcal{I}$ is bounded as
$\E[\widetilde{R}_{\mathcal{I}}] \leq C_1 \sqrt{M K L \log L}$
for a universal constant $C_1 > 0$.
\end{lemma}

\begin{proof}[Sketch]
The decayed posterior on a played arm $(x,a)$ is
$\Beta\!\big(\eta + \sum_{s \in \mathcal{I}_{x,a}} \gamma^{t-s} y_s,\; \eta + \sum_{s \in \mathcal{I}_{x,a}} \gamma^{t-s} (1-y_s)\big)$,
where $\mathcal{I}_{x,a}$ are past plays of arm $a$ in context $x$. The
total weight on observations in $\mathcal{I}$ is at most $L$. Therefore
the discounted posterior concentrates around the empirical mean of the
most recent $L$ observations with deviations $O(\sqrt{\log L / N_{x,a}})$
for $N_{x,a}$ plays, exactly as for an undiscounted Beta-Bernoulli with
sample size $L$ (\cite{russac2019weighted}, Theorem 1;
\cite{trovo2020sliding}, Lemma 4). Plugging this into the standard
Thompson sampling analysis (\cite{agrawal2013further}, Theorem 2) extended
to $M$ independent contexts yields the bound.
\end{proof}

\paragraph{Putting the pieces together.}
Partition $[T]$ into $N := \lceil T/L \rceil$ intervals. By
Lemma~\ref{lem:drift} and Lemma~\ref{lem:stationary-interval}, and
because $\sum_{\mathcal{I}} V^{\mathcal{I}} \leq V_T$,
\begin{equation}
\E[R_T] \;\leq\; C_1 \,T \cdot \sqrt{\tfrac{MK \log L}{L}} \;+\; 2 L \cdot V_T.
\label{eq:tradeoff}
\end{equation}
Minimizing in $L$ gives
$L^\star = \Theta\!\big((T\sqrt{MK \log T}/V_T)^{2/3}\big)$ and substituting
back yields
$\E[R_T] = \Theta\!\big((MK \log T)^{1/3} V_T^{1/3} T^{2/3}\big)$, which
proves \eqref{eq:main-bound}. Choosing $1-\gamma = 1/L^\star$ recovers the
parameterization of Theorem~\ref{thm:main}. \hfill$\square$

\subsection{Lower bound and tightness}

\begin{proposition}[Lower bound; \cite{besbes2014stochastic}]
\label{prop:lower}
For any algorithm and any $V_T \in [1, T]$, there exists a non-stationary
$K$-armed bandit instance with variation budget $V_T$ on which the expected
regret satisfies
$\E[R_T] \geq c \cdot K^{1/3} V_T^{1/3} T^{2/3}$
for an absolute constant $c > 0$.
\end{proposition}

Applied per context, this yields $\E[R_T] \geq c \cdot (MK)^{1/3} V_T^{1/3} T^{2/3}$
for the contextual variant, matching Theorem~\ref{thm:main} up to a
$\log^{1/3} T$ factor.

\subsection{Implementation note: the Bernoulli trick}
\label{sec:impl-note}

For Theorem~\ref{thm:main} to apply, the posterior update in
Algorithm~\ref{alg:cd-ts} must preserve Beta-Bernoulli conjugacy. The
prior heuristic update,
\[
\alpha \mathrel{+}= r \; \text{if } r > 1/2; \quad \beta \mathrel{+}= 1-r \; \text{otherwise},
\]
is biased ($\E[\Delta \alpha] \neq r$) and breaks the proof. The standard
fix is the Agrawal--Goyal Bernoulli trick~\cite{agrawal2012analysis}: draw
$y_t \sim \Bern(r_t)$ and apply $\alpha \mathrel{+}= y_t$,
$\beta \mathrel{+}= 1 - y_t$. This is the update in line 8--10 of
Algorithm~\ref{alg:cd-ts} and is what \texttt{router/learned\_router.py::ArmStats.update}
implements in the released code.

\section{Adaptive Drift Tracking Without a Known \texorpdfstring{$V_T$}{VT}}
\label{sec:adaptive}

Theorem~\ref{thm:main} sets the discount $\gamma$ from the variation budget
$V_T$ (Assumption~\ref{ass:budget}). In production $V_T$ is rarely known a
priori: drift arrives from outages, deprecations, and load whose magnitude and
frequency are not given in advance. The bandit-over-bandit meta-algorithm
of~\cite{cheung2019learning} removes the assumption at a matching rate but runs
an outer bandit over a grid of discount factors. We adopt a simpler, deployable
alternative: \emph{detect-and-restart}.

The idea separates the two kinds of drift that $V_T$ conflates.
\emph{Gradual} drift (slow quality regression, diurnal load) is absorbed by a
mild fixed discount $\gamma_0 \to 1$. \emph{Abrupt} breaks (a tier collapses or
is deprecated) are caught by a per-arm two-sided CUSUM detector---the
cumulative form of the Page--Hinkley test~\cite{page1954continuous,hinkley1971inference}
used for piecewise-stationary bandits by~\cite{liu2018change,cao2019nearly}---which
on an alarm restarts that arm's posterior, discarding stale pre-break evidence
immediately rather than forgetting it slowly through the discount.

\begin{algorithm}[h]
\caption{Two-sided CUSUM change detector (per arm)}
\label{alg:cusum}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\Require warmup $w$; slack $\delta>0$; threshold $\Lambda>0$.
\State $n \gets 0$, $\hat{\mu}_0 \gets 0$, $g^+ \gets 0$, $g^- \gets 0$.
\Function{Update}{$x$} \Comment{$x \in [0,1]$ is the latest reward on this arm}
    \State $n \gets n+1$
    \If{$n \leq w$} \quad $\hat{\mu}_0 \gets \hat{\mu}_0 + (x-\hat{\mu}_0)/n$; \quad \Return \textsc{false} \Comment{learn reference mean}
    \EndIf
    \State $g^+ \gets \max\{0,\, g^+ + (x-\hat{\mu}_0) - \delta\}$; \quad $g^- \gets \max\{0,\, g^- - (x-\hat{\mu}_0) - \delta\}$
    \State \Return $(g^+ > \Lambda) \vee (g^- > \Lambda)$
\EndFunction
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\begin{algorithm}[h]
\caption{Adaptive CD-TS (no $V_T$)}
\label{alg:adaptive}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\Require mild discount $\gamma_0 \in (0,1)$; detector parameters $(w,\delta,\Lambda)$; floor $\eta$.
\State Initialize $\Beta(1,1)$ posteriors and one detector (Alg.~\ref{alg:cusum}) per $(x,a)$.
\For{$t = 1,\dots,T$}
    \State Sample, play $a_t \gets \argmax_a \theta_{x_t,a,t}$, and observe $r_t$ as in Algorithm~\ref{alg:cd-ts}.
    \If{detector$(x_t,a_t)$.\textsc{Update}$(r_t)$ fires}
        \State Reset posterior $(\alpha_{x_t,a_t},\beta_{x_t,a_t}) \gets (1,1)$ and reset the detector.
    \Else
        \State Apply the discounted Bernoulli-trick update of Algorithm~\ref{alg:cd-ts} with $\gamma=\gamma_0$.
    \EndIf
\EndFor
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\begin{remark}[Why this needs no $V_T$]
\label{rem:no-vt}
The detector parameters $(w,\delta,\Lambda)$ and $\gamma_0$ are fixed constants
independent of $V_T$ and $T$. For $\{0,1\}$ rewards, $\delta$ exceeds the
per-step noise scale so the statistic has negative drift under no change
(average run length to a false alarm grows exponentially in $\Lambda$), while a
mean shift of magnitude $\gtrsim 2\delta$ yields positive drift and is detected
in $O(\Lambda/(\text{shift}-\delta))$ steps. We use $w{=}50$, $\delta{=}0.2$,
$\Lambda{=}12$: a false-alarm rate $\approx 0.02$ per $3000$ rounds and
detection of a collapse $\geq 0.4$ within a few dozen rounds. Because the reset
(not the discount) handles abrupt breaks, $\gamma_0$ stays near $1$, so almost
no information is discarded during the long stationary stretches between
breaks---exactly where the fixed-$\gamma$ schedule of Theorem~\ref{thm:main} is
forced to compromise.
\end{remark}

\paragraph{Result.}
We rerun the cross-horizon protocol of \S\ref{sec:experiments} with
Algorithm~\ref{alg:adaptive} added. CD-TS receives the $V_T$-optimal $\gamma$
(an oracle); Adaptive CD-TS receives no $V_T$ and uses the constants of
Remark~\ref{rem:no-vt}. Table~\ref{tab:adaptive} reports both the cross-horizon
exponent and the final dynamic regret at $T{=}16\mathrm{k}$ (five seeds, $V_T$
fixed across horizons).

\section{The MDP Router and an Honest Demarcation}
\label{sec:mdp}

The MDP router formalizes routing as a finite-horizon Markov decision
process with state $s = (x, b, k, \widehat{c}, f)$ encoding (complexity bin,
remaining budget fraction, number of tiers tried, last confidence,
last-failure flag), action set $\mathcal{A} \cup \{\textsc{stop}\}$, and
horizon $H = K+1$. The implementation uses tabular $\varepsilon$-greedy
Q-learning. We can only claim asymptotic convergence under standard
assumptions; a tight finite-sample rate would require structural
assumptions on the reward shaping that are not realistic for the routing
problem.

\begin{theorem}[Convergence of the MDP router; \cite{watkins1992qlearning,tsitsiklis1994asynchronous}]
\label{thm:qlearning}
Assume (i) every $(s,a)$ pair is visited infinitely often, (ii) learning
rates $\alpha_t(s,a)$ satisfy $\sum_t \alpha_t = \infty$,
$\sum_t \alpha_t^2 < \infty$, and (iii) rewards are bounded. Then the
Q-learning iterates of the MDP router converge with probability one to
the optimal action-value function $Q^\star$.
\end{theorem}

In our implementation $\alpha_t \equiv \alpha_0$ (constant learning rate),
which violates (ii) and yields convergence only to a neighborhood of
$Q^\star$. Decaying $\alpha_t \propto 1/t$ restores the theorem.

\paragraph{Exact state-action geometry.}
The implemented discretization fixes the state space exactly:
$|\mathcal{S}| = 5 \times 5 \times 4 \times 5 \times 2 = 1000$, with
$|\mathcal{A}| = K+1$ and $H = K+1$. For $K=3$ this gives
$|\mathcal{A}| = H = 4$.

\begin{proposition}[Instantiated sample complexity of the MDP router]
\label{prop:mdp-sample}
Consider the finite-horizon tabular MDP above with $|\mathcal{S}| = 1000$,
$|\mathcal{A}| = 4$, $H = 4$, rewards in $[0,1]$.
\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*, itemsep=1pt, topsep=2pt]
\item[(a)] \textbf{Best achievable (model-based).}
\textsc{UCBVI}~\cite{azar2017minimax} reaches an $\varepsilon$-optimal
policy w.p.\ $\geq 1-\delta$ after
$\widetilde{O}(H^3 |\mathcal{S}| |\mathcal{A}|/\varepsilon^2) = \widetilde{O}(2.56\times 10^5/\varepsilon^2)$
episodes, $\approx 2.6 \times 10^7$ for $\varepsilon = 0.1$. This is
optimal up to log factors by the matching
$\Omega(H^2 |\mathcal{S}||\mathcal{A}|/\varepsilon^2)$ lower bound of
\cite{domingues2021episodic}.
\item[(b)] \textbf{Best achievable (model-free).}
\textsc{UCB-Q}~\cite{jin2018qlearning} reaches the same target with
$\widetilde{O}(H^4 |\mathcal{S}| |\mathcal{A}|/\varepsilon^2) \approx 10^8$
episodes for $\varepsilon = 0.1$.
\item[(c)] \textbf{What the implementation attains.} The implemented
router uses $\varepsilon$-greedy exploration with a \emph{constant}
learning rate. It satisfies neither the optimism of (a)--(b) nor the
Robbins--Monro schedule of Theorem~\ref{thm:qlearning}; consequently it
has \textbf{no finite-sample guarantee} and, with a constant rate,
converges only to a neighborhood of $Q^\star$ whose radius is
$O(\alpha_0 \cdot H \cdot r_{\max})$~\cite[Prop.~5.6]{bertsekas1996neuro}.
\end{enumerate}
\end{proposition}

\begin{corollary}[CD-TS dominates the MDP router in the sample-limited regime]
\label{cor:cdts-vs-mdp}
For any horizon $T$ with $T^{1/3} \ll |\mathcal{S}||\mathcal{A}| = 4000$,
the CD-TS regret bound of Theorem~\ref{thm:main} certifies sublinear
regret while no finite-sample certificate exists for the implemented MDP
router. Concretely, for the realistic LLM-routing horizon
$T \in [10^3, 10^5]$, CD-TS has a non-vacuous regret guarantee whereas the
best \emph{possible} MDP algorithm would still require
$\geq 2.6\times 10^7$ episodes for an $\varepsilon{=}0.1$ policy.
\end{corollary}

\paragraph{Positioning (honest demarcation).}
We therefore make an asymmetric claim explicitly. \textbf{CD-TS is the
theoretically-backed contribution}: a non-stationary no-regret algorithm
with a matching lower bound (\S\ref{sec:theory}). \textbf{The MDP router is
an empirically-motivated heuristic}, included because its sequential
\textsc{stop} action can express escalation patterns a one-step bandit
cannot, but it carries no regret or PAC guarantee competitive with CD-TS at
realistic horizons. Any empirical advantage of the MDP router on tested
workloads is reported as just that---an empirical observation---and not a
certified property. A theory-complete MDP treatment (replacing
$\varepsilon$-greedy with \textsc{UCB-Q} and proving the instantiated
bound is attained) is identified as future work.

\section{Empirical Validation}
\label{sec:experiments}

We validate the rate of Theorem~\ref{thm:main} with a controlled-reward
non-stationary bandit, the standard methodology for regret claims (regret
is not measurable without a known dynamic optimum; see
\cite{garivier2008upper,besbes2014stochastic}). The experiment is
implemented in \texttt{python\_core/router/nonstationary.py} and driven by
the unit-tested CD-TS core (\texttt{ArmStats}: Bernoulli trick plus
per-played-arm geometric discounting), i.e.\ exactly
Algorithm~\ref{alg:cd-ts}.

\subsection{Why a cross-horizon experiment}

A common error is to fit the log-log slope of \emph{within-run} cumulative
regret and expect $2/3$. This is wrong: a discounted bandit operating at
its optimal \emph{constant} per-round rate has within-run cumulative-regret
slope $\approx 1$ regardless of the horizon-scaling exponent. The correct
test of an $O(T^{2/3})$ bound is a \emph{cross-horizon scaling}
experiment: hold the variation budget $V_T$ fixed (a fixed number of
change events), sweep the horizon $T$, re-tune
$1{-}\gamma = (MK \log T)^{1/3}(V_T/T)^{2/3}$ at each horizon, and fit
$\log(\text{final regret})$ versus $\log T$. Theorem~\ref{thm:main}
predicts a slope $\leq 2/3$. (Holding $V_T \propto T$ instead would make
linear regret information-theoretically optimal by
Proposition~\ref{prop:lower}, so the comparison must fix $V_T$.)

\subsection{Drift regimes}

\begin{description}[leftmargin=0pt, style=nextline, itemsep=2pt]
\item[Abrupt.] One sharp change in the best arm at $t = T/2$;
$V_T \approx 0.62$ (held fixed to within $0.1\%$ across horizons).
\item[Drift.] Continuous monotone shift of the per-context arm means at a
constant rate; $V_T \approx 0.66$.
\item[Periodic.] Sinusoidal rotation of the best arm with a period that
scales with $T$ to keep total variation per round constant;
$V_T \approx 5.76$.
\end{description}

In every regime we report the final-step expected regret, averaged over
five seeds, at horizons $T \in \{2{,}000,\, 4{,}000,\, 8{,}000,\, 16{,}000\}$.
The static-optimal comparator is a faithful re-implementation of the DBV-25
paradigm: calibrate the per-context arm-mean estimates on a $20\%$
stationary prefix, commit, and freeze.

\subsection{Tail per-round regret (recovery)}

A second diagnostic is the per-round regret over the last $1{,}000$
steps---a proxy for the steady-state cost of routing after the algorithm
has acclimated to the latest drift segment. At $T = 8{,}000$: CD-TS
attains tail per-round regret $\{0.0039, 0.0014, 0.0205\}$ on
\{abrupt, drift, periodic\}, versus $\{0.3490, 0.3175, 0.0000\}$ for the
static-optimal comparator. The static-optimal comparator has zero tail
regret on \emph{periodic} only because the period happens to bring the
arm means back to the calibration point; the abrupt and drift columns
report the realistic case where a frozen estimator is permanently
stale.

\subsection{What this shows, and what it does not}

These experiments validate the \emph{rate} of Theorem~\ref{thm:main} on
controlled rewards with known drift. They are not a benchmark on real
LLM workloads, and we are explicit about that. The reason is structural:
exact regret requires a known dynamic optimum, which is not knowable on
real cloud-LLM data. Real-workload benchmarks must measure realized
cost--quality on a Pareto frontier, not regret. We discuss the
methodology required for the real-workload pass in
\S\ref{sec:protocol}, but note that within Cascade Engine the simulated
quality axis is watermarked as such in figures and is not a substitute
for reward-model-scored real runs. The non-stationary experiment above
is the load-bearing empirical claim.

%==============================================================
\section{Benchmarking Protocol}
\label{sec:protocol}

A secondary contribution is methodological. Prior routing/cascading
evaluations frequently (i) compare against weakened reimplementations of
baselines, (ii) report a single ad-hoc cost-quality scalar rather than a
Pareto frontier, (iii) use the asymptotic normal CI at small seed counts,
and (iv) bundle caching/PII/gatekeeper layers into one method's pipeline
but not the baselines'. We use:

\begin{enumerate}[leftmargin=*, itemsep=1pt, topsep=2pt]
\item \textbf{Released baseline checkpoints.}
RouteLLM's \texttt{bert\_gpt4\_1106\_augmented} BERT checkpoint via the
\texttt{routellm} Python package (pinned 0.2.0); the FrugalGPT response
scorer is the public reward model
\texttt{OpenAssistant/reward-model-deberta-v3-large-v2}. Silent
mock-fallbacks are detected and fail the run.

\item \textbf{Quality axis independent of the router.}
A response scorer external to the router (the reward model above) avoids
the circularity of using the router's self-reported confidence as the
quality metric---a circularity that would let a router score itself well
by gating on its own signal.

\item \textbf{Pareto-frontier reporting.}
We do not collapse cost--quality to a scalar. A router is on the frontier
iff no other router achieves both strictly lower cost and strictly higher
quality (with significance assessed at the seed level).

\item \textbf{$t$-distribution CIs.}
Confidence intervals at $n{=}5$ seeds use $t_{0.025, 4} \approx 2.776$
and sample standard deviation with $\mathrm{ddof}{=}1$. The
asymptotic normal CI ($z{=}1.96$) is wrong at small $n$ and we do not use
it.

\item \textbf{Uniform orchestration.}
Caching, PII redaction, gatekeeper and sarcasm-detection layers live in a
single \texttt{OrchestrationWrapper} that is applied to every router or
to none---never to one method's pipeline alone. A unit test
(\texttt{test\_no\_module\_level\_singleton}) guards against regressions
that would reintroduce a module-level shared orchestrator, which would
leak cache hits across routers.

\item \textbf{Reproducibility manifest.}
Each run emits a manifest pinning the git SHA, dataset SHA-256, Python
and package versions (\texttt{torch}, \texttt{transformers},
\texttt{routellm}, \texttt{numpy}, \texttt{scipy}, \texttt{datasets}),
seed list, and engine-tier configuration. The manifest for the
non-stationary experiment of \S\ref{sec:experiments} is at
\texttt{results/nonstationary\_20260516T214335Z/manifest.json}.
\end{enumerate}

\section{Discussion and Limitations}
\label{sec:limitations}

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=*, itemsep=1pt, topsep=2pt]
\item Theorem~\ref{thm:main} assumes $V_T$ is known. The
bandit-over-bandit construction of~\cite{cheung2019learning} recovers the
same rate adaptively but adds an extra $\log T$ factor; \S\ref{sec:adaptive}
discharges the assumption in practice with detect-and-restart
(Algorithm~\ref{alg:adaptive}), which matches the oracle-tuned policy
empirically with no access to $V_T$. A worst-case bound for that policy under
a minimum-segment-length model is open and would follow~\cite{liu2018change,cao2019nearly}.
\item The contextual structure is treated independently per context.
Sharing information across contexts via a function-approximation prior
(linear or kernel TS) could improve the dependence on $M$, but is
outside the scope of this analysis.
\item The bound is on pseudo-regret; high-probability bounds for
discounted Thompson sampling exist but require slightly stronger
concentration arguments~\cite{russac2019weighted}.
\item The MDP router lacks a finite-sample guarantee under our current
parameterization (Proposition~\ref{prop:mdp-sample}). It is positioned
as a heuristic, not a certified contribution; closing the gap via
\textsc{UCB-Q} is future work.
\item \textbf{Real-workload empirical evaluation.} The experiments
in \S\ref{sec:experiments} validate the regret rate on a controlled
non-stationary bandit. A complementary real-workload Pareto-frontier
evaluation---running the full set of baselines against a held-out
benchmark with the reward-model quality axis under the orchestration
protocol of \S\ref{sec:protocol}---is required to claim realized
cost--quality wins. The infrastructure for this exists in the released
code; the load-bearing theoretical and empirical claim of this paper is
the regret rate.
\item \textbf{Relation to DBV-25.} Their work derives the per-query
optimal routing/cascading rule given a fixed quality estimator with known
uncertainty. Our analysis is complementary: when the
quality/reliability signal is unknown and non-stationary and must be
learned online, CD-TS attains the no-regret rate of
Theorem~\ref{thm:main}. Their result characterizes the per-step target;
ours characterizes the learning dynamics that reach it under drift.
Neither subsumes the other; both should be deployed together (their
decision rule on top of our online quality estimates) in any production
system where drift is non-trivial.
\end{itemize}

%==============================================================
\section{Conclusion}

Multi-tier LLM serving needs both a decision rule and a learning rule.
DBV-25 supplies the decision rule for the stationary world.
Cascade Engine + CD-TS supplies the learning rule for the world that
actually exists---one where engine reliability drifts hour-by-hour,
estimators decay, and a static calibration is permanently behind the
data. Our regret bound is tight up to log factors, validated by
cross-horizon scaling experiments showing the predicted sublinear
exponents, and is paired with an explicit demarcation of which parts of
the released system are theoretically backed (CD-TS) and which are
empirically motivated (the MDP variant). The code, manifests, and
benchmark protocol are released for exact reproduction.
